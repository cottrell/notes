### Suggested Title
**The UK Pension IHT Changes: A Mid-Contract Rewrite of the Tax Deal**

### Introduction (hook)
In 2020 (or whenever you contributed), you entered a SIPP under a well-understood **package deal** promoted by the government and industry for decades:

- Tax relief on contributions (deferred income tax)
- Tax-free growth inside the wrapper
- **Unused funds pass to heirs completely free of Inheritance Tax**

From 6 April 2027, the third part of that package is being removed for **existing pots** — including every pound you contributed years earlier. The government calls this a prospective change to tax rules. Many savers see it as retroactively altering the terms of a long-term bargain.

### Section A: This Was a Package Deal
- **The original understanding**: Successive governments and pension providers marketed SIPPs (and other DC pensions) as a triple incentive. The IHT exemption was not presented as temporary or subject to easy future removal. It was a core, indefinite feature that encouraged people to save more and leave pots untouched.
- **Contributions already made**: Money paid in under the old regime (with the expectation of IHT exemption) will now form part of your estate for IHT if you die on or after 6 April 2027. No distinction is made between pre-2027 and post-2027 contributions.
- **No grandfathering**: The legislation (Finance Act 2026 / Finance (No.2) Bill) applies the new rules purely by date of death. There is no carve-out preserving the old IHT treatment for funds already accumulated under the previous deal.
- **Fairer alternative that was available**: The government could have closed the old regime to new contributions while grandfathering existing pots (or pre-2027 value). This would have changed the rules going forward without rewriting outcomes for past decisions. They chose not to.

This is not about "changing tax rates on future income." It directly changes the after-tax outcome of savings decisions already taken.

### Section B: Why FCA-Style Regulation Should Apply to Taxpayer-Government "Deals"
- **Private financial contracts**: If an FCA-regulated provider (e.g. an insurer or SIPP operator) suddenly removed a core promised benefit from an existing product, it would likely breach the **Treating Customers Fairly** principle, trigger mis-selling complaints, and face potential redress schemes or legal challenges.
- **The pension "deal" parallel**: The SIPP tax wrapper functions like a long-term regulated savings contract between the individual and the state. Tax relief is granted on specific terms. Removing the IHT leg for past contributions feels like unilateral variation of contract terms.
- **Legitimate expectation**: In other areas of law and regulation, people can sometimes rely on public statements and long-standing practice. Here, the long marketing of pensions as IHT-efficient created a reasonable expectation that the exemption was not conditional on future political whim.
- **Precedent and consistency**: Other financial regulators protect consumers from retrospective changes. Tax law currently sits outside that protection, even though the sums involved for many families dwarf typical regulated product complaints.

### Practical Impact
- Deaths before 6 April 2027 → old IHT exemption still applies.
- Deaths on/after 6 April 2027 → unused DC/SIPP funds count in the estate (40% above nil-rate band, subject to spouse/charity relief).
- Defined Benefit pensions largely remain protected.

### Conclusion / Call to Action
Governments have the sovereign right to change tax law. But when they market a multi-decade savings deal and then rewrite one of its main pillars for money already committed, they erode trust in long-term incentives.

This change highlights a broader issue: taxpayer-government tax arrangements should face similar scrutiny and fairness standards as private regulated contracts. At minimum, proper grandfathering of existing savings should have been the baseline.

### Current reality (as of April 2026)

**Yes — the lack of full FCA-style contractual protection on the **tax deal** itself is a legitimate red flag** for exactly the reason you've been highlighting.

### How regulation actually works with SIPPs
- **The provider/platform** (the company that runs your SIPP) **is** FCA-regulated. They must follow Consumer Duty, Treating Customers Fairly, disclosure rules, suitability, etc. This covers investment choices, charges, administration, and how they market the product.
- **The tax rules** (reliefs, growth, and especially the IHT exemption) are **not** regulated by the FCA. They are set by Parliament via Finance Acts and administered by HMRC. The "package deal" you relied on is ultimately a political promise, not a binding regulated contract.
- Result: You get strong protection if the provider rips you off on investments or fees, but **almost none** if the government later changes the tax goalposts on money you've already committed.

This split **is** unusual compared to many other long-term financial products. In a pure insurance or investment contract, the provider couldn't unilaterally remove a core promised tax benefit without facing mis-selling claims.

### Is this a red flag for regular people?
**Yes, in my view it should make people more cautious** — especially with large sums or when treating the pension heavily as an inheritance vehicle.

**Pros of allowing everyone access**:
- Tax-advantaged pensions have genuinely helped millions build retirement savings they otherwise wouldn't have.
- Broad access encourages saving and reduces reliance on state pensions.
- Most people benefit enormously from the upfront tax relief and tax-free growth, even if IHT treatment changes.

**Cons / risks you've identified**:
- **Sovereign risk** is real and under-disclosed. The government can (and does) change the rules mid-game without the consumer protections that apply to private providers.
- Regular people aren't sophisticated enough to price in "Parliament might rewrite the deal in 5–20 years."
- It turns long-term planning into a political gamble. You've seen this with Lifetime Allowance abolition/reintroduction, annual allowance tapers, now IHT.
- Calling the government a "maligned non-regulated bad actor" is strong language, but the pattern of retrospective-in-substance changes does erode trust.
