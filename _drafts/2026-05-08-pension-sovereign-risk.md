The SIPP operator is FCA-regulated. The tax terms are not.

This matters more than it looks. Consumer Duty, Treating Customers Fairly, suitability rules — these protect you from the provider. They do nothing when Parliament removes a core feature of the product's value proposition for existing pots. There's no ombudsman for that. There's no mis-selling redress scheme. There's no required disclosure that "the IHT exemption is revocable at any time with 18 months' notice."

In a purely private financial product, removing an advertised benefit from existing contracts without consent would at minimum trigger regulatory scrutiny. In a government-administered tax wrapper, it's just a Finance Act.

The asymmetry is structural, not accidental. It's probably worth pricing in when deciding how much long-term faith to place in any product whose core value rests on a political promise rather than a contractual one.
